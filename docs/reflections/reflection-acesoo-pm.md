## The Hardest Part: Coordinating Everyone While Understanding Everything

The most difficult challenge I faced as Project Manager was not managing timelines or GitHub boards — it was the constant pressure of needing to understand every part of the project well enough to coordinate it effectively. Each member owned a specialized role: the Developer was deep in Supabase and Next.js, the KM Analyst was grounding everything in SECI theory, the Designer was producing wireframes with KM rationale, and the QA Lead was writing and executing test cases. As PM, I was not the expert in any of these — but I had to understand all of them well enough to ask the right questions, spot when something was falling behind, and connect the dots between roles when they needed to align.

This meant I could never just manage the board and call it done. I had to actively learn what each deliverable looked like so I could tell when it was actually complete versus just submitted. That gap between "knowing enough to coordinate" and "knowing everything deeply" was where the hardest decisions lived — like whether to push back on a deliverable's quality or trust that a member knew their domain better than I did.

## Handling a Critical Blocker: Covering for QA

The most significant blocker we hit during the project was when our QA Lead's PC broke down during Build Sprint 1 and 2, right when test case execution was supposed to begin. Testing couldn't wait — the Developer needed bug reports to close issues before deployment, and the whole sprint timeline depended on QA completing their run.

Rather than let the work stall while waiting for the situation to resolve, I stepped in directly. I reviewed the test cases that had already been written and executed them myself against the dev branch, recording pass/fail results in `/docs/test-cases/` in the format the guidelines required. At the same time, the UX/UI Designer was also under pressure finalizing the Peer Support Stories wireframes, so I helped review and give feedback on the designs to keep that moving in parallel.

It was not the cleanest solution — I was context-switching between PM duties, QA testing, and design review all at once — but the alternative was letting two parts of the sprint slip simultaneously. The experience reinforced that a PM's job is ultimately to protect the team's output, even when that means temporarily absorbing work outside your defined role.

## What I Would Do Differently

If I were to manage this project again, I would be more hands-on and more strict from day one — not in a way that micromanages people, but in a way that sets clearer expectations earlier. Looking back, some of the scrambling in later sprints could have been avoided if I had read the professor's guidelines more thoroughly at the start and made sure every member understood not just their tasks, but the specific standards those tasks had to meet.

The guidelines were detailed — from the exact format of ADR entries to the required 11 sections of the README to the naming convention for the KM report file. These details mattered during the defense, and I should have extracted them into a shared team checklist from Week 1 rather than discovering gaps under pressure near submission. Being strict early is not about being difficult — it is about making the final week less stressful for everyone.
