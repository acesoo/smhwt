# KM Analyst Reflection
## Student Mental Health & Wellness Tracker — Sprint 5
**Author:** Anthon Van B. Calban (@anthoncalban)
**Role:** Knowledge Management Analyst

---

When I was assigned the KM Analyst role, I understood it as primarily a documentation task — writing the theoretical sections of a report that would sit alongside the "real" technical work happening in the codebase. What this project revealed to me is that knowledge management is not documentation. It is architecture. Every decision I made about tag vocabulary, taxonomy structure, and retrieval requirements had a direct, material consequence on what the developer could build and how the application would actually function.

The clearest example of this was the S3-KM-03 audit. When I reviewed enzo-q's migration files against `km-architecture.md`, I found that `stressor_tags`, `coping_tags`, and `tags` were all unconstrained free-text arrays. From a technical standpoint, the schema looked complete — the columns existed, the tables were structured correctly. But from a KM standpoint, the Combination stage had been rendered non-functional: without a controlled vocabulary, the dashboard could never aggregate entries by tag because the same concept could be expressed in infinitely many different strings. Filing that GitHub Issue and seeing enzo-q implement the fix — the `valid_tags` lookup table, the CHECK constraints, the GIN indexes — was the moment I understood that KM decisions have technical consequences. The taxonomy I had designed from academic sources was not just a section in a report; it was a database schema requirement.

The framework decision that I am most confident about is choosing the SECI Model over Communities of Practice for the individual self-tracking layer. The research I conducted on stigma and labeling effects (Huang & Bashir, 2017) gave me a principled, evidence-based argument for why a communal framework is structurally inappropriate for mental health data — not because community is bad, but because the psychological barriers to candid self-disclosure in a communal space are precisely the conditions we were trying to remove. The subsequent addition of the Peer Support Stories feature actually strengthened this argument: by applying Communities of Practice only at the anonymous peer layer, we ended up with a dual-framework architecture that is more theoretically coherent than a single-framework approach would have been. Socialization happens through peer stories; Externalization, Combination, and Internalization happen privately. The two layers are complementary because they operate on different types of knowledge under different conditions of disclosure.

If I were to approach this project differently, I would research the **limitations of controlled vocabulary systems** earlier. The tag taxonomy I designed is research-grounded and academically validated, but it is also static. I only addressed this limitation in the final sprint's Limitations section, when in practice it should have shaped the taxonomy design from the beginning — perhaps by designing a mechanism for students to suggest new tags that could be reviewed and added to the vocabulary, rather than treating the initial list as permanent. A vocabulary that cannot evolve will eventually fail to represent the experiences of its users, and in a mental health context, that failure has consequences beyond data integrity.

The most important thing I will carry from this project is the concept of the Ba. Nonaka's argument — that knowledge creation does not happen in a vacuum but requires an enabling environment specifically designed for it — applies as much to software architecture as it does to organizational theory. We did not build a mood tracker. We built a knowledge space. The difference matters.
